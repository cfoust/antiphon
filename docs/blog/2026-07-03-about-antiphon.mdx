Somewhere along the way, working with coding agents turned into watching them. A good
session now means four or five of them running at once — one refactoring, one writing docs,
one chasing a flaky test — and me, cycling through terminal tabs like a security guard
checking monitors. The agents got faster and more capable every month. My side of the
arrangement stayed the same: poll, squint, scroll.

{/* truncate */}

That's a strange way to supervise collaborators, when you think about it. Nobody manages a
team by staring at all of them simultaneously. You share a space. You develop peripheral
awareness. You hear the tone of the room — who's in flow, who's stuck, who just pushed back
from their desk with something to say. Vision is a foreground sense; it needs you to point
it. Hearing is the background one. It monitors everything, all the time, for free.

So I built the thing I wanted: a room.

<Eye />

Antiphon gives every agent session a voice and a seat in a virtual space around you. It's a
native macOS app doing real binaural rendering — measured HRTFs, room acoustics, a webcam
watching your head so the world holds still while you move. An agent isn't a tab anymore;
it's a presence two meters off your left shoulder. When it runs tools, you hear its chord
tick downward, note by note. When it settles into a long build, it drops to a low
three-tone machine hum, the way you'd hear a lathe from across a workshop. When it
finishes, it tells you — a sentence or two, in its own voice, from its own spot in the
room.

The interaction I care most about is the one that costs nothing: **close your eyes.** The
camera notices. The desk fades, the room comes up, and for a few seconds you're standing
among your agents. Turn toward the voice that wants you, hold your gaze a beat, and it
delivers its report. Open your eyes and there's a reply box waiting — type, enter, and
you're back to your own work. Triage by ear, ten seconds, no tabs.

> Nothing pings. Nothing flashes. A waiting agent builds a slow bloom in one ear —
> over minutes — until you have a moment for it.

That gentleness is the design center, and it's the part I had to relearn the most. Every
notification system I've ever used treats attention as something to *seize*. Antiphon
treats it as something you *lend*. The sounds took dozens of rounds of tuning-by-ear to get
right — quieter, lower, slower, always in that direction — because the failure mode of an
ambient tool isn't subtlety, it's annoyance. If you notice the sound design, it's wrong.

## The engineering bet

Under the hood there's one bet, made early: a single Rust DSP core, no I/O, no threads, no
allocation on the audio path, compiled twice — a staticlib inside the Mac app and a
WebAssembly module for the browser. The two builds are verified byte-identical, which is
why [the web demo](https://antiphon.dev/demo.html) isn't a mockup of the product; it *is*
the renderer, running scripted agents instead of yours. Even the demo film on the front
page was scored offline through the real engine.

The other thing that turned out to matter more than expected: head tracking beats HRTF
personalization. Fancy per-ear measurement is the thing everyone asks about, but motion is
what convinces your brain that a voice is out there — the soundfield counter-rotating
against your head, quickly and smoothly. One fit slider and a webcam get you almost all the
way.

A small Go daemon ties it together: agent plugins connect on localhost, sessions get
persistent personas and voices (ElevenLabs or OpenAI if you have keys, the macOS voices if
you don't), and everything streams to the app. Claude Code, Codex, OpenCode, Pi, and Aider
have adapters today, and the protocol is deliberately small — an adapter is an afternoon.

## The name

*Antiphon* — ἀντίφωνον — is the old word for call-and-response singing: voices answering
each other across a space. That's the shape of the work now, and it deserved better than a
dashboard. The whole thing is [open source, MIT](https://github.com/cfoust/antiphon).

Put on headphones. [Close your eyes.](https://antiphon.dev/)
